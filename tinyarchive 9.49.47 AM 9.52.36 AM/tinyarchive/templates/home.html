Introduction

Our Digital Archive brings in the power of preservation to exquisite pieces of crafted currency, in an effort to reflect back and appreciate fine art. From coins that are exchanged in the marketplace to high worth valuables, feel free to check out our archive to marvel at some these excellent pieces. Each high resolution image gives us insight into the coin's exact size, condition, and material used for an in depth analysis. Even if you are not a coin collector, each of our coins can help you learn a different part of US History.

Our Mission

The United States Currency Archive aims to identify rare coins and denominations from past United States history. We strive to find coins with designs and patterns that are different then the more popular coins in circulation. We organized these coins from our personal collections, and researched the historical context and meaning behind them.

Motivation for the Project

We believe that physical monetary objects keep becoming a relic of the past, and having a collection will be fruitful in reminding us of history. Coins and dollars are symbols of the US economy, and in the age of growing credit/debit NFC payments we notice that the sentiment associated with physical money has slowed down. We wanted to learn the several different stories that come with each dollar bill and coin, like how Andrew Jackson (a former slave owner) has been used on the $20 bill since 1928.

Project Process

Our project was created using frameworks, and tools taught in the Digital Humanities 150A class at UC Berkeley taught by Professor Johnston. We utilized the Django development server to host our artifact data. Using these digital tools we were able to explore Digital Humanities concepts, primarily based upon figuring out the historical political context behind certain design choices. Each symbol on the coins represents a different theme in history, with some coins being more controversial than others.

What We Learned

Aside from learning about the history and signficance in a poltical context of every coin, we learned quite a bit from the development journey. When embedding images, we struggled with resizing and preserving the resolution of the image. On our archive, some of images are lower resolution, but once you click on them they expand to full size where each detail of the coin is visible. Using HTML & CSS took a bit of patience, and we would have to troubleshoot for short streches trying to figure why the site is not updating the way as intended.
